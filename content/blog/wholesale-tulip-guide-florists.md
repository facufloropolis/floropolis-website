# Wholesale Tulips for Florists: Varieties, Season, and Sourcing Guide

Tulips are one of the most profitable seasonal flowers in a florist's inventory. They're affordable wholesale ($0.40-2.50/stem), universally loved by consumers, and have a distinctive look that no other flower replicates. During peak season (January-April), tulips can represent 15-25% of a shop's revenue.

This guide covers tulip varieties, sourcing strategy, care, and how to maximize profit during tulip season.

## Care and Handling

### The Tulip Rules

Tulips are unique among cut flowers — they keep growing after cutting. This affects everything about how you handle them.

**At receiving:**
1. **Unpack immediately** — tulips are ethylene sensitive
2. **Cut stems straight** (not at 45 degrees — tulips are monocots)
3. **Place in COLD water** (34-38°F ideal) with flower food
4. **Use shallow water** — 2-3 inches only. Deep water causes stem softening
5. **Store COLD** — 34-38°F cooler is perfect
6. **Keep upright** — tulips are phototropic (bend toward light) and gravitropic (bend upward)
7. **Wrap loosely in paper** if you need them to stay straight in cooler

### Key Care Points

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Stem growth** | Tulips grow 1-2 inches after cutting. Account for this in arrangements |
| **Phototropism** | Bend toward light. Rotate displays. In arrangements, accept the natural movement |
| **Water consumption** | Heavy drinkers. Check water daily — tulip arrangements go dry fast |
| **Temperature** | Cold extends life. Warm opens blooms quickly. Use cold storage for inventory, warm display to open for sale |
| **Daffodil toxicity** | NEVER mix tulips with daffodils/narcissus in water. Daffodils release a sap that kills tulips. Keep separate for 24+ hours before combining |
| **Expected vase life** | 5-10 days (standard), 7-12 days (French), depends heavily on temperature |

### Common Problems

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Drooping heads | Normal tulip behavior (not wilting) | Let them be — most clients love the natural droop |
| Stems splitting | Forced opening in warm water | Keep in cool water, handle gently |
| Leaves yellowing | Too warm, too long in storage | Use fresh stock, keep cold |
| Premature opening | Display too warm or too much light | Move to cooler area, reduce light exposure |
| Stem bending | Phototropism | Wrap in paper for first 4 hours, or embrace the curve |

## Design Applications

### Spring Collections
- **"Just tulips" bouquet:** 15-20 stems of one color, wrapped simply. The minimalist bestseller every spring
- **Mixed spring:** Tulips + ranunculus + anemones + hyacinth = the ultimate spring arrangement
- **Ombré tulip design:** 5 varieties in graduating colors (white → pink → deep pink → magenta → red)

### Weddings
- **Spring bridal:** French tulips have the drama for bridal bouquets (March-May weddings)
- **Centerpiece:** Tulips in low arrangements — their natural droop creates organic, garden-style feel
- **Ceremony:** Mass plantings in pots along the aisle (return to wholesaler or resell after)

### Everyday
- **Grab-and-go wraps:** 10 standard tulips, paper-wrapped. $15-25 retail from $4-8 cost. Spring impulse buy
- **Subscription:** "Weekly spring tulips" add-on during season. 10 stems/week, 12 weeks = $300-600 revenue per subscriber

### Valentine's Day
- **Rose alternative pitch:** "Looking for something different? Our French tulips are elegant, unique, and last just as long"
- **Mixed Valentine's:** Tulips + roses + ranunculus in reds and pinks. Tulips reduce your cost while adding variety

## Seasonal Strategy

### Pre-Season (September-November)
- Lock in wholesale contracts for January-April supply
- Plan Valentine's Day tulip offerings
- Pre-sell spring wedding tulip packages

### Peak Season (January-April)
- Feature tulips prominently in-store and online
- Run "Tulip Tuesday" weekly promotions
- Stock 3-5 varieties minimum at all times
- Push French and parrot tulips as premium upgrade

### Post-Season (May)
- Final "last tulips of the season" push (scarcity drives urgency)
- Transition displays to peonies, garden roses, and summer flowers
- Review what varieties sold best — inform next year's orders
