# Hiring for Your Flower Shop: When, Who, and How to Build Your Team

Every flower shop owner reaches the same point: you're doing everything — designing, delivering, sourcing, selling, answering phones, cleaning, posting on Instagram — and you're burning out. It's time to hire.

But hiring wrong is expensive. A bad hire costs 30-50% of their annual salary when you factor in training, lost productivity, and replacement. This guide covers when to hire, who to hire first, and how to build a team that grows your business.

## When to Hire

### The Signal It's Time

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|-------------|
| You're turning away orders | Revenue loss > cost of hiring |
| Your vase life is suffering (waste up) | No time for proper flower care |
| You haven't taken a day off in months | Burnout = mistakes = client loss |
| Instagram/marketing is stalled | No time for growth activities |
| Wedding leads take 3+ days to respond | Losing bookings to faster competitors |
| You're delivering AND designing | Can't do both well simultaneously |

### The Math

**Hire when the cost of NOT hiring exceeds the cost of hiring.**

Example:
- You're turning away 3-4 orders/week because you're at capacity
- Average order: $65
- Lost weekly revenue: $195-260
- Monthly lost revenue: $780-1,040
- Part-time assistant (15 hrs/week × $16/hr): $960/month

If hiring frees you to capture that lost revenue PLUS improve quality and marketing, the hire pays for itself immediately.

## Who to Hire First

### The Hiring Priority Stack

| Hire # | Role | When | Why First |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| 1st | **Delivery driver / shop assistant** | When you can't both design and deliver | Frees you to focus on revenue-generating design work |
| 2nd | **Part-time designer / assistant** | When you need help with production | Doubles your design output for peak days |
| 3rd | **Sales / customer service** | When phone and email are overwhelming | Ensures leads don't go unanswered |
| 4th | **Full-time designer** | When you have consistent volume | Allows you to step into management/growth |
| 5th | **Manager** | When you're managing people full-time | Frees you to run the business, not work in it |

### Role Descriptions and Pay

| Role | Type | Hourly Rate | Annual (FT) | Key Skills |
|------|------|-----------|------------|-----------|
| Delivery driver | PT/FT | $14-18/hr | $29K-37K | Clean driving record, customer-facing, punctual |
| Shop assistant | PT | $14-17/hr | — | Reliable, can process flowers, clean, answer phone |
| Junior designer | PT/FT | $16-20/hr | $33K-42K | Basic design skills, takes direction well |
| Experienced designer | FT | $20-28/hr | $42K-58K | Independent design, can handle weddings/events |
| Lead/head designer | FT | $25-35/hr | $52K-73K | Manages design team, client-facing, mentors juniors |
| Shop manager | FT | $22-30/hr | $46K-62K | Operations, staffing, inventory, customer service |

**Note:** Pay varies significantly by city. NYC, LA, and SF are 20-40% above these ranges. Rural areas may be 10-20% below.

## Interview Process

### For Designers: Design Test

The most important part of a designer interview isn't the conversation — it's watching them work.

**Design test setup:**
1. Provide a selection of 15-20 stems (roses, filler, greenery) + a vase
2. Ask them to make an arrangement "as if a customer ordered a $60 birthday arrangement"
3. Give them 20-30 minutes
4. Observe: Do they start with greenery? How do they handle stems? Is the arrangement balanced? How fast do they work?

**What to look for:**
- Clean technique (proper cuts, no crushed stems)
- Color sense and balance
- Speed (experienced designers work fast)
- Confidence vs. hesitation
- How they handle the stems (gentle? rough?)

### Interview Questions

| Question | What You're Assessing |
|---------|---------------------|
| "Why do you want to work with flowers?" | Passion and motivation |
| "What's your availability, including weekends and holidays?" | Valentine's Day and Mother's Day are non-negotiable workdays |
| "Tell me about a time you handled a difficult customer" | Customer service skills |
| "How do you handle working under pressure?" | Peak holiday readiness |
| "What's your experience with [POS system / delivery software]?" | Technical skills |
| "Are you comfortable with social media?" | Instagram/TikTok capability |

### Red Flags

- **"I don't work weekends."** Weekends are peak flower days. Non-negotiable
- **"I've worked at 4 shops in 2 years."** High turnover = reliability issue
- **Can't name any flowers beyond roses and tulips.** Lack of basic knowledge (OK for drivers, not for designers)
- **Negative about previous employers.** They'll be negative about you next
- **No questions about the role.** Lack of genuine interest

## Onboarding and Training

### Week 1: The Basics

| Day | Training Focus |
|-----|---------------|
| Day 1 | Shop tour, intro to team, basic procedures, POS system |
| Day 2 | Flower processing: unboxing, cutting, conditioning, storage |
| Day 3 | Arrangement basics: your shop's style, customer favorites |
| Day 4 | Customer service: phone, in-person, delivery etiquette |
| Day 5 | Independent work with supervision. Review and feedback |

### Month 1: Building Competence

- Shadow experienced designers on wedding/event work
- Learn the ordering system and inventory management
- Practice 2-3 of your most popular arrangements until consistent
- Handle customer interactions with a senior team member nearby
- Learn the delivery route and procedures

### Ongoing Training

- **Weekly:** 15-minute design session (demonstrate a technique, have them replicate)
- **Monthly:** Product knowledge (new seasonal flowers, care tips)
- **Quarterly:** Industry trends, social media training, business goals update

## Managing a Flower Shop Team

### Scheduling

| Season | Staff Level | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Jan-March (slow) | Minimum staff | Cross-train, deep clean, plan |
| April-May (Mother's Day) | Full + temp | Hire temp help 3-4 weeks before |
| June-Sept (wedding season) | Full staff | Prioritize wedding team availability |
| Oct-Nov (moderate) | Standard | Prep for holidays |
| Dec (holiday) | Full + temp | Corporate events, holiday orders |
| Feb 1-14 (Valentine's) | ALL HANDS | Everyone works. Plan 6 weeks early |

### Retention

The #1 reason florists quit isn't pay — it's burnout and feeling undervalued.

| Retention Strategy | Cost | Impact |
|-------------------|------|--------|
| Competitive pay (review annually) | Variable | High |
| Flexible scheduling (when possible) | Free | High |
| Design freedom (let them create) | Free | Very high |
| Professional development (workshops, certifications) | $200-500/year | High |
| Recognition (credit their work on Instagram) | Free | High |
| Clear growth path (assistant → designer → lead) | Free | Very high |
