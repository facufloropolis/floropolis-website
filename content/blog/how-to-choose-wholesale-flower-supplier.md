# How to Choose a Wholesale Flower Supplier: 7 Things Every Florist Should Check

Your wholesale flower supplier determines your quality, your margins, and your stress level. Get this right and your business runs smoother. Get it wrong and you're dealing with short vase life, inconsistent colors, surprise price jumps, and emergency runs to the market at 5 AM.

Whether you're a new florist choosing your first supplier or an established shop evaluating alternatives, here are the 7 things that actually matter.

## 1. Freshness (How Many Days Since Harvest?)

This is the single most important factor and the one most florists don't ask about.

**The question to ask:** "How many days from harvest will my flowers be when they arrive?"

| Source Type | Typical Days from Harvest | Vase Life Remaining |
|-------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Farm-direct | 3-4 days | 12-14 days |
| Regional distributor | 5-7 days | 8-10 days |
| Local wholesale market | 7-10 days | 5-7 days |

**Why it matters financially:** Fresher flowers = less waste. Industry average waste is 15-25%. Farm-direct suppliers report client waste rates of 8-12%. On $2,000/month in purchases, that difference is $100-260/month saved — $1,200-3,100 per year.

**What to look for:**
- Supplier can tell you the exact harvest-to-delivery timeline
- They ship temperature-controlled from origin
- Stems arrive hydrated and firm, not soft or discolored

## 2. Pricing Transparency

**The question to ask:** "Can I see all your prices before I order?"

The wholesale flower industry has a transparency problem. Many suppliers:
- Quote different prices to different buyers
- Add fees that aren't in the per-stem price (fuel surcharges, handling fees, box fees)
- Change prices weekly without notice
- Require you to call for pricing on "market rate" items

**What a good supplier looks like:**
- Published prices visible online or in a catalog
- Per-stem pricing that includes delivery (no hidden fees)
- Price changes communicated in advance
- No "call for pricing" on standard items

**Red flags:**
- "Prices vary" without explanation
- Minimum purchase requirements that force you to buy more than you need
- Different prices for the same item depending on when you ask

## 3. Variety and Selection

**The question to ask:** "How many varieties do you stock, and which ones are consistently available?"

| Supplier Type | Typical Variety Count |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Local wholesale market | 50-100 (what fits in their cooler) |
| Regional distributor | 80-150 |
| Farm-direct (Ecuador/Colombia) | 200-300+ |

**Why variety matters:**
- Wedding clients request specific varieties and colors
- Seasonal trends change what customers want
- Offering unique varieties (tropicals, specialty stems) differentiates your shop
- More options = more creative freedom in your designs

**What to check:**
- Do they have the specific varieties you use most?
- Are those varieties consistently available, or frequently out of stock?
- Do they carry specialty and tropical flowers, or just basics?

## 4. Order Minimums and Flexibility

**The question to ask:** "What are your minimum order requirements?"

| Minimum Type | What It Means for You |
|-------------|---------------------|
| No minimum | Order what you need, when you need it |
| Per-variety minimum (e.g., 25 stems) | Reasonable for most florists |
| Dollar minimum (e.g., $100-500) | Can force you to overbuy |
| Box minimum (e.g., must buy full boxes) | Often means 100-200+ stems of one variety |

**For smaller shops and home-based florists:** High minimums are a deal-breaker. You can't use 200 stems of one variety before they wilt. Look for suppliers with low per-variety minimums (10-25 stems) or no minimum on mixed orders.

**For event florists:** Box quantities can work in your favor — you'll use 200+ stems for a single wedding. But even event florists need flexibility for variety.

## 5. Delivery Reliability

**The question to ask:** "What's your on-time delivery rate, and what happens when something goes wrong?"

**What to check:**
- How long does delivery take? (Same-day? 2 days? 4 days?)
- Is delivery included in the price or an additional charge?
- Do they deliver on weekends?
- What happens if an order arrives damaged or late?
- Do they have a substitution policy if a variety is unavailable?

**The planning trade-off:** Farm-direct requires 4-6 days lead time but delivers maximum freshness. Local distributors offer same-day but shorter vase life. Most florists use both — farm-direct for planned orders, local for emergencies.

**A supplier's response to problems tells you everything.** Good suppliers: acknowledge immediately, offer credit or replacement, have a clear process. Bad suppliers: blame the farm, blame shipping, make you fight for a credit.

## 6. Consistency

**The question to ask:** "Will the flowers I order next month look the same as what I order today?"

Consistency matters more than peak quality. A supplier who delivers "great" one week and "mediocre" the next is worse than one who delivers "solid" every single time.

**What affects consistency:**
- **Single-source suppliers** (one farm or farm network) tend to be more consistent than aggregators who buy from whoever's cheapest that week
- **Named varieties** should look the same order to order (a Vendela rose should always be a Vendela rose)
- **Stem length** should match what you ordered (don't accept 40cm when you ordered 60cm)

**How to test:** Order the same variety 3 weeks in a row. Compare stem length, bloom size, color, and vase life. Consistent? Good supplier. Variable? Proceed with caution.

## 7. Communication and Support

**The question to ask:** "If I have an issue at 7 AM on delivery day, who do I contact and how fast will they respond?"

This is the factor that separates good suppliers from great ones.

**What great support looks like:**
- Responsive within hours (not days) during business hours
- A named contact person who knows your account
- Proactive communication about delays or availability issues
- Willing to help you plan orders for events (not just take orders)
- Available via the channel you prefer (email, phone, WhatsApp)

**What poor support looks like:**
- Voicemail that never gets returned
- Different person every time you call
- You find out about stock-outs when your order doesn't arrive
- "We'll get back to you" and they don't

## The Evaluation Checklist

Before committing to a supplier, score them on each factor:

| Factor | Questions to Ask | Score (1-5) |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Freshness** | Days from harvest? Vase life on arrival? | ___ |
| **Pricing** | Published prices? Hidden fees? Delivery included? | ___ |
| **Variety** | How many varieties? Specialty/tropical available? | ___ |
| **Minimums** | Per-variety minimum? Box-only? Dollar minimum? | ___ |
| **Delivery** | Lead time? On-time rate? Weekend delivery? | ___ |
| **Consistency** | Same quality week to week? Named varieties? | ___ |
| **Support** | Response time? Named contact? Proactive communication? | ___ |
| **TOTAL** | | ___/35 |

**30-35:** Excellent — lock this supplier in
**25-29:** Good — use as primary but keep a backup
**20-24:** Adequate — use as secondary/emergency source
**Below 20:** Look elsewhere

## How to Test a New Supplier

Don't switch all your ordering at once. Follow this process:

1. **Week 1:** Place a small trial order (your most-used 3-5 varieties)
2. **Week 2:** Evaluate quality, freshness, accuracy, delivery time
3. **Week 3:** Place a second order with more variety. Track vase life
4. **Week 4:** Compare pricing, waste rate, and margin to your current supplier
5. **Decision:** If the trial scores higher on most factors, start shifting your planned orders

**The low-risk way to start:** Most good suppliers offer a free or low-cost sample or trial order. Take advantage of it. There's no reason to commit without testing.
