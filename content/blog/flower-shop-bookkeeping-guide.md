# Flower Shop Bookkeeping: Financial Management for Florists

Most florists didn't start a flower business because they love spreadsheets. But the florists who stay in business? They understand their numbers.

This guide covers the financial basics every florist needs: what to track, how to track it, and what the numbers should look like if your business is healthy.

## Why Florist Bookkeeping Is Different

Flower shops have unique financial challenges that most small businesses don't:

1. **Perishable inventory.** You can't sell yesterday's wilted roses tomorrow. Waste is a constant cost
2. **Cash flow timing.** You pay for flowers before they sell (often 7-14 days before revenue arrives)
3. **Seasonal extremes.** Valentine's and Mother's Day can be 30-40% of annual revenue
4. **Mixed revenue streams.** Retail, weddings, events, subscriptions — each with different margins
5. **High COGs.** Flowers + hard goods can be 40-55% of revenue, leaving thin margins for error

## The Financial Statements You Need

### 1. Profit & Loss (P&L) — Monthly

This tells you: "Am I making money?"

| Category | % of Revenue | Example ($15K/mo) |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Revenue** | 100% | $15,000 |
| Cost of Goods Sold (flowers + hard goods) | 40-50% | $6,750 (45%) |
| **Gross Profit** | 50-60% | $8,250 (55%) |
| Rent/lease | 8-15% | $1,800 (12%) |
| Labor (including yourself) | 15-25% | $3,000 (20%) |
| Delivery/vehicle | 3-5% | $600 (4%) |
| Marketing | 3-5% | $450 (3%) |
| Insurance | 1-2% | $225 (1.5%) |
| Utilities + tech | 2-3% | $375 (2.5%) |
| Supplies + misc | 2-3% | $300 (2%) |
| **Total Operating Expenses** | 35-55% | $6,750 (45%) |
| **Net Profit** | 8-15% | $1,500 (10%) |

**Healthy benchmarks:**
- Gross margin: 55-65% (below 50% = sourcing problem)
- Net margin: 8-15% (below 5% = expense problem)
- Owner compensation: Included in labor. If not, your "profit" is actually your salary

### 2. Cash Flow Tracker — Weekly

Cash flow kills more flower shops than lack of sales. You can be "profitable" on paper and still run out of cash.

**Weekly cash flow template:**

| Week | Cash In | Cash Out | Net | Running Balance |
|------|---------|----------|-----|----------------|
| Week 1 | $3,200 | $4,100 | -$900 | $8,100 |
| Week 2 | $3,800 | $3,200 | +$600 | $8,700 |
| Week 3 | $4,100 | $3,500 | +$600 | $9,300 |
| Week 4 | $3,900 | $3,800 | +$100 | $9,400 |

**The danger zone:** If your running balance dips below 2 weeks of operating expenses, you need to act immediately (cut costs, accelerate collections, or arrange a credit line).

### 3. Inventory Waste Report — Weekly

Track what you throw away. This is the silent killer of flower shop margins.

| Week | Total Purchased | Total Sold/Used | Waste | Waste % | $ Lost |
|------|----------------|----------------|-------|---------|--------|
| Week 1 | $1,200 | $1,020 | $180 | 15% | $180 |
| Week 2 | $1,100 | $990 | $110 | 10% | $110 |

**Target:** Under 15% waste. Under 10% is excellent. Over 20% = you're over-ordering or not selling fast enough.

## Setting Up Your Books

### Software Recommendations

| Tool | Cost | Best For |
|------|------|---------|
| **QuickBooks Online** | $30-80/mo | Most flower shops. Industry standard, accountant-friendly |
| **Wave** | Free | Startup/solo florists on a tight budget |
| **Xero** | $15-75/mo | International businesses, multi-currency |
| **FreshBooks** | $19-60/mo | Service-based (mostly weddings/events) |

**Recommendation:** QuickBooks Online Simple Start ($30/month). It handles everything a flower shop needs and every accountant knows it.

### Chart of Accounts for Florists

Set up these categories in your accounting software:

**Revenue:**
- Retail flower sales
- Wedding/event revenue
- Subscription revenue
- Corporate account revenue
- Delivery fees
- Workshop/class revenue

**Cost of Goods Sold:**
- Fresh flowers (wholesale)
- Hard goods (vases, ribbon, foam, wire)
- Packaging (boxes, wrap, tissue)

**Operating Expenses:**
- Rent/lease
- Payroll + owner's draw
- Delivery/vehicle (gas, maintenance, insurance)
- Marketing + advertising
- Insurance (general liability)
- Utilities (electric, water, internet)
- Software + POS subscriptions
- Professional services (accountant, legal)
- Supplies (cleaning, office)
- Continuing education + industry events

### The 15-Minute Weekly Routine

Every week (Friday or Monday), spend 15 minutes on:

1. **Categorize transactions** (5 min) — Match bank transactions to categories in your software
2. **Review cash position** (3 min) — How much cash do you have? How much do you owe this week?
3. **Check waste** (3 min) — What got thrown out? Why?
4. **Review outstanding invoices** (2 min) — Who owes you money? Follow up if overdue
5. **Glance at P&L** (2 min) — Are you on track for the month?

## Tax Deductions for Florists

These are the deductions florists most commonly miss:

### Commonly Missed Deductions

| Deduction | Category | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Vehicle mileage (deliveries) | Delivery | $0.67/mile in 2026. Track every delivery mile |
| Home office (if home-based) | Rent | Percentage of home used exclusively for business |
| Flower waste/shrinkage | COGS | Documented waste is deductible |
| Industry conferences | Education | AIFD, SAF events, workshops |
| Business insurance | Insurance | General liability, vehicle, property |
| Website + software | Tech | POS, accounting, email, design tools |
| Professional photography | Marketing | Product photos, wedding portfolio |
| Business meals (50%) | Marketing | Client consultations over coffee/lunch |
| Continuing education | Education | Floral design courses, business coaching |
| Bank + processing fees | Fees | Credit card processing (typically 2.5-3.5%) |
| Uniforms/branded apparel | Supplies | If it has your logo and you wear it for work |

### Quarterly Tax Obligations

If you're a sole proprietor or LLC, you likely owe quarterly estimated taxes:
- **Q1:** Due April 15
- **Q2:** Due June 15
- **Q3:** Due September 15
- **Q4:** Due January 15

**Rule of thumb:** Set aside 25-30% of net profit for taxes. Put it in a separate savings account so you don't spend it.

### Sales Tax

Most states require you to collect sales tax on retail flower sales. Key things:
- **Get your reseller's permit** — This lets you buy wholesale without paying sales tax (you collect it from the end customer instead)
- **Services may be taxed differently** — Delivery fees, setup fees, and labor may or may not be taxable depending on your state
- **File on time** — Late sales tax filing comes with penalties in every state
- **Check your state rules** — Sales tax varies dramatically by state and even by city

## Managing Seasonal Cash Flow

The flower business has extreme seasonality. Here's how to manage it:

### Revenue Distribution by Month (Typical Flower Shop)

| Month | % of Annual Revenue | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| January | 5% | Slow. Plan, organize, reduce staff hours |
| February | 15-18% | Valentine's Day = biggest single day |
| March | 6% | Spring starts, wedding consultations pick up |
| April | 7% | Easter, spring events |
| May | 12-15% | Mother's Day = biggest revenue week |
| June | 8% | Wedding season begins |
| July | 6% | Weddings, summer events |
| August | 5% | Slow summer. Plan for fall |
| September | 6% | Back to school, events resume |
| October | 6% | Fall events, Halloween |
| November | 7% | Thanksgiving, holiday prep |
| December | 10-12% | Holiday season, corporate events |

### The Seasonal Cash Flow Strategy

1. **Build a cash reserve in peak months.** Save 20% of February and May revenue for slow months
2. **Reduce variable costs in slow months.** Cut staff hours, order fewer flowers, reduce marketing spend
3. **Use slow months for projects.** Website updates, process improvements, wedding consultations for next year
4. **Don't expand during peak months.** The revenue feels great, but it's temporary. Make expansion decisions based on annual averages
5. **Line of credit.** Establish a $5,000-10,000 business line of credit when you don't need it. You'll need it in January

## Pricing Sanity Checks

Run these checks monthly to make sure your pricing is healthy:

### The 3× Rule
If your average retail bouquet sells for $65 and your average flower cost per bouquet is $20, your markup is 3.25×. That's healthy.

If your markup drops below 2.5×, either your prices are too low or your flower costs are too high.

### The Labor Rule
Your total labor cost (employees + your own reasonable salary) should be 20-30% of revenue. Above 30% and you're overstaffed or underpriced. Below 15% and you're burning yourself out.

### The Rent Rule
Rent should be under 15% of revenue. Above 15%, your location is too expensive for your sales volume.
