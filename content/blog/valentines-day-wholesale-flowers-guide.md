# Valentine's Day Wholesale Flowers: The Florist's Complete Planning Guide

Valentine's Day is the #1 flower holiday. Period. Americans spent over $2.3 billion on Valentine's flowers in 2025, and that number grows every year. For florists, February 14th is either the most profitable day of the year — or the most stressful. The difference is preparation.

This guide covers everything you need to plan your V-Day wholesale inventory: what to order, when to order it, how much, and how to price for maximum profit.

## The V-Day Timeline: When to Do What

**8 weeks out (December 15):**
Start planning. Review last year's V-Day sales data. Estimate this year's demand.

**6 weeks out (January 5):**
Place your rose order with your farm-direct supplier. Ecuador farms allocate V-Day inventory starting in early January. Late orders get short stems or no allocation.

**4 weeks out (January 19):**
Order specialty stems and greens. Confirm all quantities with your supplier.

**2 weeks out (February 1):**
Finalize order. Confirm delivery dates. Order hard goods (vases, boxes, ribbon, wrapping).

**1 week out (February 7):**
Confirm all shipments. Prepare your workspace. Pre-make anything that can be assembled early (bows, gift tags, packaging).

**3 days out (February 11):**
Receive farm-direct flowers. Begin processing and storing. Start pre-making wrapped dozens.

**Day before (February 13):**
This is your biggest production day. Have everything prepped, labeled, and ready for delivery and walk-ins.

**Day of (February 14):**
Sell. Deliver. Celebrate.

## How Much to Order

### The Rule of Thumb

**Take your average weekly rose sales and multiply by 5-10x.** That's your Valentine's Day target.

### Volume Guide

| Shop Size | Normal Weekly | V-Day Target | Rose Stems Needed | Wholesale Budget |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Home-based | 50 stems | 250-500 | 200-400 roses + accents | $250-500 |
| Small shop | 200 stems | 1,000-2,000 | 800-1,600 roses | $800-1,600 |
| Medium shop | 500 stems | 2,500-5,000 | 2,000-4,000 roses | $2,000-4,000 |
| Large shop | 1,000+ stems | 5,000-10,000 | 4,000-8,000 roses | $4,000-8,000 |

**Order 30-40% more than you think you need.** It's better to have 50 extra dozens than to run out at 2 PM on Valentine's Day. Leftover roses sell the next week at normal price — running out loses the biggest revenue day of the year.

## V-Day Pricing Strategy

### Mark Up Aggressively (Clients Expect It)

Valentine's Day is the one time of year where clients expect to pay more for flowers. Don't feel guilty about V-Day pricing — your costs are higher too (premium wholesale prices, overtime labor, delivery logistics).

| Product | COGS | Suggested V-Day Price | Margin |
|---------|------|---------------------|--------|
| Single rose (wrapped) | $1.50 | $8-12 | 81-88% |
| Half dozen red roses | $9 | $35-50 | 74-82% |
| Dozen red roses (wrapped) | $14-16 | $60-85 | 77-81% |
| Dozen red roses (vased) | $22-28 | $85-120 | 74-77% |
| Premium mixed arrangement | $25-35 | $95-150 | 74-77% |
| Luxury tropical arrangement | $30-45 | $120-200 | 75-78% |

### Offer 3-4 Tiers

Don't make clients choose from 20 options. Offer a clear menu:

1. **Classic** — Dozen red roses, wrapped ($65-75)
2. **Premium** — Dozen roses with accent flowers, wrapped or boxed ($85-100)
3. **Luxury** — 2 dozen roses or mixed premium arrangement, vased ($120-150)
4. **Ultimate** — Custom luxury arrangement ($175-250+)

Most clients will choose #2 or #3. This is called the "decoy effect" — the Classic makes Premium look like good value.

### Pre-Order Discounts

Offer a 10% discount for orders placed before February 7. This helps you:
- **Plan production better** (know your order volume in advance)
- **Reduce waste** (make what's ordered, not what you guess)
- **Collect payment early** (cash flow before the event)
- **Reduce day-of stress** (less walk-in chaos)

## Production Planning

### The Night Before (February 13)

This is the real Valentine's Day for florists. Plan for a 12-16 hour production day.

**Pre-make everything possible:**
- Wrapped dozens (your #1 seller) — process, wrap, label, refrigerate
- Boxed arrangements — assemble, refrigerate
- Delivery orders — fully assembled, labeled with delivery address and time
- Walk-in inventory — have 2-3x your expected walk-in volume ready to grab

### Delivery Strategy

| Option | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Own delivery | Control, personal touch | Limited capacity, vehicle wear |
| Partner with delivery service | Scale, no vehicle needed | Less control, cost per delivery |
| Pickup only | Zero delivery cost | Limits your market |
| Mix (own + partner) | Best of both worlds | Coordination complexity |

**The math on delivery:** If you charge $15/delivery and it takes 30 minutes round-trip, you're making $30/hour on delivery labor. Worth it for orders over $50 (good margin). Not worth it for single-rose orders.

## Common V-Day Mistakes

1. **Under-ordering roses.** The #1 mistake. Order 30-40% more than your estimate.
2. **Waiting too long to order.** By late January, premium varieties are allocated. Order in early January.
3. **Underpricing.** V-Day clients expect premium prices. Don't discount.
4. **No pre-order system.** If you only sell walk-in, you're leaving money on the table and creating unnecessary stress.
5. **Forgetting the day after.** February 15th is NOT a day off. You'll have leftover roses to sell, delivery follow-ups, and thank-you messages to send to every client.
