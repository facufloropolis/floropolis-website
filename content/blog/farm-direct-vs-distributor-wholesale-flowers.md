# Farm-Direct vs Distributor: How Wholesale Flower Sourcing Actually Works

If you're a florist, you've probably ordered from a wholesale distributor — someone like a local flower market, a regional wholesaler, or a national online distributor. That's how most florists buy flowers. It's familiar, it's fast, and it works.

But there's another option that's becoming more common: ordering farm-direct. Buying wholesale flowers straight from the farms in Ecuador and Colombia, with no middlemen between the grower and your shop.

This article breaks down the real differences — not marketing spin, but the actual trade-offs — so you can decide which sourcing method works for your business.

## How the Supply Chain Works

### The Distributor Path (Traditional)

```
Farm → Exporter → Importer → Distributor → Your Shop
```

**Timeline:** 7-10 days from harvest to your cooler
**Hands touched:** 3-4 intermediaries
**Each step adds:** cost (margin), time (transit + storage), and freshness loss

Here's what happens at each step:
1. **Farm harvests** and packs stems into boxes
2. **Exporter** consolidates orders from multiple farms, ships to US
3. **Importer/broker** receives at Miami (or other port), stores in cold chain facility
4. **Distributor** buys from importer, stores in their own cooler, sells to florists
5. **You** pick up from the wholesale market or receive delivery

By the time stems reach you, they've been cut 7-10 days ago, stored in 2-3 different coolers, and handled by multiple people. Vase life remaining: typically 5-7 days.

### The Farm-Direct Path

```
Farm → Your Shop
```

**Timeline:** 3-4 days from harvest to your cooler
**Hands touched:** 1 (the farm packs and ships directly to you)
**What's eliminated:** exporter margin, importer margin, distributor margin, extra cold storage days

Your flowers arrive 3-4 days after harvest with 12-14 days of vase life remaining. That's the entire difference in one sentence.

## The Comparison: What Actually Matters

### 1. Freshness & Vase Life

This is the single biggest difference and the one that impacts your bottom line most directly.

| Metric | Farm-Direct | Distributor |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Days from harvest to you | 3-4 days | 7-10 days |
| Typical vase life remaining | 12-14 days | 5-7 days |
| Waste rate (unsold stems) | 8-12% | 15-25% |

**Why this matters financially:**

If you buy 100 stems from a distributor and 20% go to waste, you paid for 100 but only sold 80. Your effective cost per sold stem just went up 25%.

If you buy 100 stems farm-direct and 10% go to waste, you paid for 100 and sold 90. Your effective cost per sold stem went up only 11%.

Even if farm-direct and distributor had identical per-stem prices (they don't — farm-direct is usually cheaper), the waste reduction alone makes farm-direct more profitable.

### 3. Variety & Selection

| Factor | Farm-Direct | Distributor |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Total varieties available | 200-300+ | 50-100 (what fits in their cooler) |
| Tropical flowers | Consistently available | Rarely stocked |
| Specialty stems | Wide selection | Limited, seasonal |
| Color consistency | Direct from named farm | Mixed sources, variable |

Distributors stock what sells fastest. That means roses, carnations, and baby's breath dominate their cooler. If you want heliconias, anthuriums, specific garden rose varieties, or less common specialty stems, you'll struggle to find them consistently at a distributor.

Farm-direct gives you access to the full catalog of what Ecuador and Colombia grow — which is the most diverse flower selection in the world.

### 4. Lead Time & Flexibility

| Factor | Farm-Direct | Distributor |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Minimum lead time | 4-6 days | Same day (market pickup) or 1-2 days (delivery) |
| Emergency orders | Not possible | Available |
| Planning required | Need to order ahead | Can be more spontaneous |

**This is where distributors win.** If you need 50 roses by tomorrow afternoon, a farm-direct service can't help you. Your local wholesale market can.

The solution most professional florists use: **farm-direct for 70-80% of planned orders** (weekly standing orders, events booked in advance, predictable retail demand) and **local distributor for 10-20% emergency needs** (last-minute orders, unexpected demand spikes, variety substitutions).

### 5. Consistency & Reliability

| Factor | Farm-Direct | Distributor |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Stem quality consistency | High (single source) | Variable (multiple sources) |
| Color matching | Reliable | Can vary batch to batch |
| Supply reliability | Weather-dependent (rare disruptions) | Same + distributor stock-outs |
| Order accuracy | High | Variable |

Farm-direct flowers come from named farms that you build a relationship with over time. The roses you order this week look like the roses you ordered last month because they're the same variety from the same grower.

Distributor flowers are sourced from whoever has the best deal that week. Quality and color can shift between orders.

## When to Use Each Source

### Use Farm-Direct For:
- Weekly standing orders (your predictable weekly inventory)
- All event and wedding flowers (plan 2-4 weeks ahead)
- Specialty and tropical stems (can't get these reliably elsewhere)
- Subscription arrangements (consistent quality matters)
- Any order you can plan 4+ days in advance

### Use a Local Distributor For:
- Emergency same-day needs
- Small quantities of common varieties
- Testing a new variety before committing to a larger farm-direct order
- Walk-in demand spikes you didn't anticipate

### The 80/20 Rule

The most profitable florists use this ratio:
- **70-80% farm-direct** — planned orders, events, subscriptions
- **15-20% local distributor** — emergencies, small restocks
- **5-10% local growers** — unique seasonal varieties, supporting local, storytelling for customers

## How to Get Started With Farm-Direct

If you're currently buying 100% from distributors and want to try farm-direct:

2. **Plan 5 days ahead.** Place your farm-direct order on Monday for Friday delivery.
3. **Keep your distributor relationship.** You still need them for emergencies. This isn't about replacing them — it's about optimizing your sourcing mix.
